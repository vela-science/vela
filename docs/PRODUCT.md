# Product

Vela is a scientific knowledge navigation system for researchers,
research operators, and AI agents working over complex scientific
evidence.

It is not a note-taking app, not a PDF chat interface, and not a
generic knowledge graph. Vela turns scattered scientific material into
a navigable state layer: findings, evidence, provenance, uncertainty,
contradictions, trails, and open frontiers.

Vela exists so science can compound.

> 借りた光、共有された発見
> *Borrowed light. Shared discovery.*

## Product register

**Primary register: product.** Design serves the work. The interface
must prioritize legibility, trust, navigation, provenance, and calm
control. It may be beautiful, but beauty must clarify rather than
decorate.

**Secondary register: brand / editorial.** Marketing surfaces, public
essays, launch pages, and artifact covers may lean more poetic.
Product UI stays precise, quiet, and operational.

## Core users

### 1. Frontier scientist

A scientist uses Vela to understand a domain, inspect evidence,
compare claims, find contradictions, identify gaps, and decide what
experiment or analysis should happen next.

They need:

- fast orientation in unfamiliar fields
- provenance they can inspect
- confidence and uncertainty without false precision
- strong literature and dataset grounding
- a way to distinguish settled findings from speculative trails

They do not need:

- another generic dashboard
- AI summaries without evidence
- decorative graph spaghetti
- inflated claims of certainty

### 2. Research operator

A program lead, lab builder, translational strategist, or scientific
founder uses Vela to map a frontier, allocate attention, evaluate
opportunities, and coordinate scientific work.

They need:

- landscape-level views
- decision trails
- portfolio / frontier maps
- gap analysis
- team-readable briefs
- exportable evidence packages

They do not need:

- toy visualizations
- abstract diagrams that cannot drive decisions
- generic productivity language

### 3. AI research agent

An AI agent uses Vela as a structured substrate for scientific
reasoning and execution.

It needs:

- stable object identities
- typed relations
- append-only provenance
- confidence and scope fields
- correction propagation
- deterministic materialized views
- machine-readable evidence bundles

It does not need:

- prose-only memory
- ungrounded chain-of-thought artifacts
- arbitrary graph schemas

## The atomic unit

The atomic unit is the **finding**, not the paper.

A finding is a structured claim with:

- statement
- evidence
- scope
- entities
- methods
- sources
- confidence
- uncertainty
- provenance
- lineage
- contradictions
- dependencies
- review and correction state

Papers, datasets, experiments, notes, and model outputs are inputs.
Findings are compiled state.

## Product primitives

### Finding

A structured scientific claim or observation.

Examples:

- "RELA pathway activation is associated with increased cellular
  resilience under oxidative stress."
- "AAV9 crosses the blood-brain barrier in juvenile mice under
  condition X."
- "Compound Y improves mitochondrial respiration in cell line Z at
  dose range D."

### Evidence

The source-backed support or opposition for a finding. Evidence
always exposes:

- source
- method
- dataset or sample context
- effect direction
- confidence contribution
- limitations

### Link

A typed relation between findings or entities. Default relation types:

- supports
- contradicts
- narrows
- extends
- depends_on
- explains
- predicts
- replicates
- fails_to_replicate
- uses_method
- measured_in
- derived_from

### Trail

A navigable reasoning, provenance, or decision path. Trails show how
a conclusion, hypothesis, experimental plan, or research direction
emerged.

### Constellation

A stable map of related findings, evidence, gaps, and trails around a
scientific question. Constellations are not just visual clusters.
They are named, inspectable knowledge structures.

### Gap

A first-class absence. A gap is an unknown, missing experiment,
unresolved contradiction, unmeasured population, incomplete mechanism,
or untested translation step.

### Correction

A structured update to previous state. Corrections must propagate
through dependencies and derived views.

## Core product surfaces

### 1. Overview

The calm cockpit. Shows the user:

- active frontier or project
- recent findings
- unresolved contradictions
- high-value gaps
- trails needing review
- new evidence affecting prior confidence
- agent activity that matters

### 2. Map

The constellation view. Shows findings, concepts, evidence, datasets,
people, and methods as a navigable scientific landscape.

The map must never become decorative graph soup. It uses progressive
disclosure, filtering, semantic zoom, clustering, and plain-language
labels.

### 3. Finding detail

The truth-inspection surface. Shows:

- finding statement
- confidence
- evidence table
- scope
- linked concepts
- provenance trail
- contradictions
- correction history
- export and share actions

### 4. Trails

The provenance and reasoning surface. Shows:

- chronological history
- source lineage
- agent and human contributions
- decisions and alternatives
- experiments proposed or completed
- state changes over time

### 5. Sources

The ingestion and source-management surface. Shows:

- papers
- datasets
- preprints
- notebooks
- protocols
- lab records
- external APIs
- ingestion quality
- extraction status

### 6. Collections

Curated scientific state for a project, program, therapeutic area, or
open problem. Collections feel like working maps, not folders.

### 7. Notes

Human-readable working memory. Notes can reference structured objects
but should not replace them.

### 8. Alerts

Signals worth attention. Alerts are sparse and meaningful:

- new contradiction detected
- high-confidence evidence added
- confidence drift changed a key finding
- dependency invalidated
- relevant dataset or paper found
- trail requires review

## Interaction principles

### 1. Show the state, then the proof

Every important statement should be inspectable. If a user sees a
conclusion, they should be one click away from source evidence,
method context, confidence basis, provenance, contradictions, and
correction history.

### 2. Progressive disclosure over maximal display

Scientific complexity is real. Vela does not hide it, but it stages
it. Default views orient. Detail views satisfy scrutiny.

### 3. Confidence is not decoration

Confidence must be tied to evidence quality, replication, recency,
method fit, sample relevance, and contradiction state. Do not show
confidence as a vanity score.

### 4. Contradictions are valuable

Contradictions are dignified, not treated as errors. They are part of
the map.

### 5. Gaps are first-class

A missing experiment can be as important as an existing finding.

### 6. Agents are participants, not magicians

AI actions are visible, attributable, reviewable, and reversible.
Never imply the AI "knows" without showing what it read or did.

## Assistant behavior

The Vela assistant behaves like a calm research navigator.

It should:

- ask grounded scientific questions
- summarize only with citations and evidence references
- explain uncertainty clearly
- propose next actions
- expose provenance
- name contradictions
- suggest trails
- help inspect a map

It should not:

- hype discoveries
- anthropomorphize excessively
- bury uncertainty
- generate fake citations
- claim consensus where none exists
- produce generic productivity advice

## Voice

The voice feels like a brilliant research navigator: composed, clear,
allergic to exaggeration.

### Use

- short sentences for UI
- direct verbs
- concrete nouns
- evidence-aware language
- precise qualifiers
- humble uncertainty

### Avoid

- inflated claims
- vague "insights" language
- "AI magic" phrasing
- excessive metaphor
- motivational copy
- exclamation points
- anthropomorphic overreach

### Banned words

`unlock`, `supercharge`, `AI-powered`, `revolutionize`, `blazing`,
`seamless` outside strict technical use, `powerful`, `next-generation`,
`cutting-edge`, `world-class`, `state-of-the-art`, `paradigm`,
`disruptive`, `seamlessly`, `effortlessly`, `10x`, `game-changing`.

### Banned punctuation

The em-dash (`—`) is a distinguishing tell of LLM-shaped writing.
Reach for the period or the colon first. The em-dash is allowed only
when no other punctuation works. The one decorative separator is `·`.

### Tone by surface

#### Product UI

Calm, operational, exact.

> 4 findings changed confidence after new evidence was added.

#### Empty states

Helpful, quiet, forward-moving.

> No trails yet. Import sources or create a trail from selected
> findings.

#### Alerts

Sparse and factual.

> New evidence contradicts one dependency in this trail.

#### Marketing

More poetic, but still disciplined.

> Science moves through what it can remember, correct, and pass on.

#### Technical docs

Normative and specific.

> Every correction event MUST reference the object it modifies and
> SHOULD include a propagation scope.

#### CLI banners

Mono eyebrow + tick row + sentence-case label, never `===` or `---`.
ANSI gated on `stdout.is_terminal()` and `NO_COLOR`. The `·` is the
only decorative separator.

### Good product copy

- "3 findings changed confidence this week."
- "This trail depends on 2 unresolved assumptions."
- "New evidence weakens the prior conclusion."
- "No contradiction found in selected sources."
- "Review provenance before exporting."
- "This claim is outside the source scope."

### Bad product copy

- "Your discovery engine is ready!"
- "Unlock breakthrough insights instantly."
- "AI has found the truth."
- "Explore the universe of knowledge!"

## Messaging architecture

### Problem

Scientific knowledge is trapped in documents, memory, and
disconnected tools. Discovery happens, but transmission fails. Claims
lose provenance. Contradictions are buried. Gaps remain unnamed. AI
agents reason over text without a durable state layer.

### Solution

Vela compiles scientific work into structured, provenance-tracked
findings and links them into navigable constellations. Scientists and
agents can inspect evidence, trace trails, detect contradictions, and
update shared state as knowledge changes.

### Why now

AI increases the value of structured scientific state. Agents cannot
safely accelerate science if they reason over unstable, untyped,
provenance-poor text. The bottleneck is no longer only reading more
papers. It is maintaining a correctable map of what science knows.

### Differentiation

Vela is not another interface over papers. It is a state layer for
findings, links, trails, confidence, corrections, and gaps.

## Quality bar

A Vela screen is successful when:

- a scientist can understand what changed
- a skeptical expert can inspect why
- a research lead can decide what to do next
- an AI agent can read and write structured state safely
- the UI feels calm under complexity
- nothing important depends on vibes alone

A Vela artifact is successful when it feels:

- calm before it feels impressive
- precise before it feels poetic
- original without being strange
- elegant without being fragile
- scientific without being sterile
- futuristic without being neon
- human without being casual

## Default build instruction for AI agents

When building Vela UI, prefer product mode. Use the design system in
`docs/DESIGN.md`. Make the interface calm, precise, and navigable.
Every claim-like element should have a path to provenance. Avoid
generic SaaS patterns, decorative graph effects, and hype language.
If uncertain, choose clarity over beauty and evidence over metaphor.

When generating Vela brand work, do not produce generic modern SaaS.
Build from the actual world of Vela: borrowed light, scientific
provenance, constellations, sails, reflection, correction, and calm
navigation. Use beauty as orientation. Use metaphor only when it
sharpens meaning. Default to restraint.
